Hira will discuss the approaches used towards verification of real-world systems, why that verification is important, and the specific verification projects currently underway at Data61.

Her perspective is highly relevant to this course, as the techniques used in such verifications are not very different from concepts we have covered. I would highly encourage all students who are able to attend.

7 What is COMP3141?

Liam O'Connor [2019-03-20 Wed 14:35]

COMP3141 has a very vague course title, so many of you may be curious as to what this course is actually about, and what it involves. Sometimes this course is erroneously called a "Haskell course". In reality, it is a software engineering course that happens to use Haskell.

By software engineering I do not mean the mixture of project management techniques and object-oriented programming and modelling taught to you in, for example, most of the software engineering workshops or COMP1531. Rather, I mean rigorous software engineering using mathematical principles.

Some of you may have learnt about formal verification or formal methods from courses such as COMP2111, SENG2011, or COMP4161. While this is an admirable pursuit, this course is not about formal verification either. Rather, we investigate the use of mathematically structured programming to deliver software that is more reliable, more verifiable, and more elegant.

Central to the notion of mathematically structured programming is the art of specification. Writing formal specifications and understanding their limitations is a central theme of this course. In the course, we will learn how to translate these specifications into meaningful artefacts that relate to a Haskell implementation, such as logical property-based tests, type-system enforced invariants, or proofs via equational reasoning. We will investigate design patterns based on mathematical ideas, and ways of thinking compositionally to produce working software in a bottom-up fashion.

Workload wise, this course is not a light-weight course, despite the absence of tutorials. There are weekly multiple-choice quizzes (the difficulty of which is no joke: you will not be able to merely copy the answers from the lecture slides), and weekly programming exercises. In addition, we have two substantial programming assignments.
